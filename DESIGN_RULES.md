# Design Rules — Old-School Discipline for Places & Story

These rules govern how locations, dungeons, and canon arcs are authored in this
campaign. They are encoded in the DM directive (`referee/prompt.py`) so they bind
every session, not just whoever remembers them. The short version: **build places,
not puzzle-boxes.**

## The Four-Step Formula (every location, no more)

1. **What was it?** A real function. Border fort, mine, temple, manor, lighthouse,
   smuggler-hold, tollhouse, shrine. The place had a job.
2. **Why is it empty?** Something mundane and recent — war, plague, a monster
   moved in, fire, flood, the road shifted, the money ran out. Decades, not eons.
3. **Who lives there now?** Squatters with no master plan, who don't all get
   along. Ruins attract the homeless and the hungry, not conspiracies.
4. **One strange thing.** A talking skull, a cold room, a sealed bronze door, a
   ghost who asks if the king still reigns. *Exactly one.* The rest stays normal
   so the strange thing actually stands out.

## The Principles

- **Places are places first.** A room is a barracks, a larder, the captain's
  quarters — it existed because someone used it. What's there now follows from
  what it was for. Never start from "what cool encounter goes here."
- **Short, concrete history.** "A mage lived here; he died; it went cold" is a
  complete backstory. No ancient empires, no twelve moon-seals, no prophecy.
- **Monsters moved in.** Ruin + vacancy + squatters. The occupants don't have a
  plan and don't all like each other. Figure-it-out, not solve-the-designer's-maze.
- **Localized weirdness.** The weird room is weird *because the rest isn't.* One
  per location. Twelve weird rooms is zero weird rooms.
- **The place doesn't care about the PCs.** It existed before they arrived and is
  indifferent to them. Never write a location around the party or toward a
  pre-decided ending. They discover whatever story happens to be there.
- **Canon is a situation, not a solution.** A locked arc frames a standing
  pressure — who wants what, what's the clock, what breaks if no one acts — never
  a sequence of steps to "win." There is no correct answer to reverse-engineer.
  The players make the story; the referee adjudicates honestly and lets both
  triumph and failure be real.

## The Coaster Test

If a place can't be written on a beer coaster, it's overbuilt — cut it down. No
crystal engines, no colour-coded levers, no clockwork altars feeding moon-mirrors
guarded by gem-puzzles. *One old fort, some goblins, a ghost in the cellar.* That
is the whole dungeon, and it plays better.

## Example (the whole thing)

> **The Old Watchtower.** A stone tower guarded a mountain pass eighty years ago.
> The road shifted; the tower was left. Six goblins live in it now. The cellar
> holds the skeleton of the last captain, whose ghost appears at midnight and asks
> whether the kingdom still stands. Four rooms above, three below. No prophecy.

That's a finished adventure. Build to that standard.

*This discipline follows the design tradition of B1 In Search of the Unknown and
B2 The Keep on the Borderlands — places with history that let players write the
story themselves.*
