# GREYWETHER GRANGE

*An old-school exploration module for AD&D 1e / OSRIC. Region: the southern hill country of the march of Halvedd, the Known World (Orruvane). Nearest town: Wend (walled caravan town; inn: the Broken Wheel), about a day's ride north. Recommended: a party of 4-6 characters of levels 1-3.*

---

## PREMISE

Greywether Grange was the seat of a minor gentry family who farmed sheep and barley in the hills above Wend and kept a few armed men to watch a back-road shortcut that let drovers skip the King's-Road toll. About seventy years ago the wool trade drifted down to the river, a hard winter and a sheep-murrain gutted the flocks, and then the last Greywether heir died without an heir of his own. Creditors squabbled over the husk for a few years and then everyone simply went away. The hills took it back. Today the walls still stand, half-strangled in bramble, and three lots of stragglers shelter inside them: a band of road-brigands in the hall, a knot of goblins that wormed up through the cellars from the caves below, and one old graveward woman who talks to the dead. The deep well, men in Wend say, sometimes whispers back. Nobody who says so will go down it.

## THE GROUPS

**The Crow's-Foot (brigands).** Eight to ten road-robbers holed up in the hall and gatehouse, led by **Mald Crow-foot**, a cashiered march-soldier who is pragmatic, tired, and not cruel for its own sake — he robs caravans on the shortcut to feed his people and would rather not fight a determined armed party. He knows the goblins are below and has a standing don't-poke-them truce; he thinks the well is haunted and forbids his men to drink from it (they haul water from a stream). **Goal:** survive the season, take one or two more fat caravans, then move on before the Halvedd reeve sends men. He will parley, hire, bluff, or bolt before he will make a last stand.

**The goblin warren.** Eight to fourteen goblins who broke up out of the natural caves into the undercroft a few years back. No chief with a master plan — a squabbling rabble under a goblin called **Skritch** who holds rank only because he has the biggest knife. **Goal:** food, shiny things, and not being noticed. They steal from the brigands' larder at night, which both sides blame on the well. They will not willingly go near the fissure (17); something about it cows them.

**Nan Tegg.** One half-mad old woman, near ninety, who has squatted here longer than anyone and tends the Greywether graves out of a loyalty no one living remembers the reason for. **Not a combatant; not a threat.** She is the module's rumor- and lore-source: she remembers the family, the murrain, the well being dug deeper "the year the whispering started." She is frightened of nothing here except the well, which she both fears and feeds (she leaves crusts at the fissure's edge "so it stays quiet"). She will talk to anyone who is gentle and shares food.

## ROOM KEY

### 1. THE GATE
**Was:** the grange's only formal entrance, an iron-shod oak gate in the curtain wall, wide enough for a hay-wain.
**Now:** one leaf hangs off a broken hinge; the other is barred from inside with a fresh-cut timber — the brigands' work. A boy lookout, **Pell** (bandit, AC 8 leather, 1 HD, **hp 4**, sling 1d4), sits in the gatehouse window above and rings a cracked handbell if he sees armed strangers. He is twelve, frightened, and will run rather than fight if rushed. Climbing the bramble-choked wall elsewhere is possible (it is 12 ft, crumbling) but noisy.

### 2. THE GATEHOUSE
**Was:** guardroom for the two or three men who watched the road, with a ladder to the wall-walk.
**Now:** the brigands' watch-post and overflow bunk. Two off-duty brigands dice here: **Dob** (AC 7 leather+shield, 1 HD, **hp 6**, short sword 1d6) and **Yannick** (AC 8 leather, 1 HD, **hp 3**, spear 1d6). A jug of sour ale, a greasy deck of cards, and a pot with **17 sp and 4 gp** thrown in as a card-pot, forgotten when a fight broke it up. Pegs hold three cloaks and a brigand's spare boots.

### 3. THE YARD
**Was:** the working yard — muck, hens, the smell of sheep, carts.
**Now:** waist-high nettle and bramble, a fallen cart, the stump of the old whipping-post. A **wild boar** has rooted a wallow in the far corner and will charge anything that blunders within 20 ft of her piglets.
> **Boar, Wild:** AC 7, HD 3+3, **hp 17**, tusk 3d4, move 150 ft, morale 75 (will not break while piglets live).
Three half-grown piglets (no stats; flee). In the bramble near the cart, dropped years ago and grown over: a **silver brooch shaped like a ram** (worth 35 gp), tangled in a dead man's belt-buckle and a few rib-bones — a creditor's hired man who lost an argument here.

### 4. THE STONE HALL
**Was:** the heart of the grange — a single great stone-floored room with a central hearth, the long table, the lord's high seat, where the household ate and the manor-court was held.
**Now:** the brigands' common hall. A fire burns in the old hearth; bedrolls line the walls; the long table survives, scarred. At most hours **Mald Crow-foot** and **two to four brigands** are here.
> **Mald Crow-foot** (leader): AC 5 (chain + shield), 3 HD, **hp 20**, long sword 1d8 (+1 to hit, strong), light crossbow 1d4. Carries a purse of **41 gp** and a **steel signet ring** (not his; pried off a merchant; worth 60 gp, and identifiable — trouble or leverage).
> **Brigands here (4):** all 1 HD. AC 7 leather+shield. **hp: 7, 5, 8, 2.** Arms: 2 short sword (1d6), 1 spear (1d6), 1 short bow (1d6).
Under a loose hearthstone (find on a search) Mald has cached the band's takings: **220 sp, 130 gp, a garnet (50 gp), and a roll of good Wend wool-cloth (worth 25 gp).** A trapdoor in the corner, half-hidden under a bedroll, leads down to the undercroft (8); the brigands keep it shut and avoid the cellar.

### 5. THE SOLAR
**Was:** the lord's private upper chamber over the hall's end — bed, shutters, a writing-desk, where the Greywethers slept and kept accounts.
**Now:** Mald keeps it as his own quarters; it is the one dry room with a sound shutter. A real bed-frame (no mattress; ropes rotted), a clothes-chest, the ruin of a writing-desk. In the chest: Mald's spare gambeson, a bottle of decent southern wine, and a **bundle of old Greywether letters and a land-deed** — worthless as coin, but they name the family, the murrain year, and a line about "the well dug to the second water, and the singing in it" (lore hook). A loose floorboard hides **2 gp and a lady's silver ear-drop (10 gp)** — older than the brigands; the last Greywether's, never found.

### 6. THE SERVANTS' QUARTERS
**Was:** a low lean-to chamber off the hall where the household servants slept on pallets.
**Now:** damp, half-collapsed roof, nettles growing through the floor at the open end. The brigands use the dry half for stores: sacks of meal, a side of smoked bacon, a barrel of apples going soft, coils of rope, two spare spears. A nest of **giant rats** has burrowed under the rotten pallets and raids the meal-sacks.
> **Giant Rats (5):** AC 7, HD 1d4, **hp: 3, 4, 2, 4, 1**, bite 1d3 (and disease, save or sicken), move 120 ft, morale 50.
Among the rat-fouled straw, a chewed leather pouch with **13 sp** and a child's clay whistle.

### 7. THE WELL  *(the one strange thing — do not explain it)*
**Was:** the grange's deep well, sunk in the yard, the only water in a dry summer; long ago it was dug deeper — "to the second water" — and broke through into a natural fissure in the rock below.
**Now:** a stone-rimmed shaft, the windlass rotted, a frayed rope still tied off. On a still night the well **whispers** — a low, wordless mutter that seems almost to answer when spoken to, rising and falling, never quite words. The brigands will not drink from it and bar their men from the yard after dark. Nan Tegg leaves bread on the rim. The goblins below will not pass beneath it. Lower a light and you see the shaft drops 60 ft to black water, with a dark slot — the fissure mouth — opening off one side about 40 ft down (a hard climb, or a way into 17). **What whispers is never resolved.** It may be wind in the fissure, an echo off water, the goblins' own muttering carried up the rock, the dead in the cellar graves, or something that was always down there. Present the sound; let the table decide. Anything thrown in is simply gone.

### 8. THE UNDERCROFT
**Was:** the vaulted storage cellar under the hall — barrels of ale, salt-meat, the manor's overflow.
**Now:** empty racks, broken hoops, the dust of seventy years, and the goblins' frontier. The trapdoor from the hall (4) comes down here; a passage at the back leads to the larder (9) and a rough-broken hole in the wall (the goblins' doing) opens into the warren (15). One goblin sentry skulks here when the warren remembers to post one (50% by day, 80% by night).
> **Goblin sentry (1):** AC 6, HD 1d8-1, **hp 4**, short sword 1d6, move 60 ft, morale 50.
A scatter of gnawed bones and, kicked into a corner, a brigand's lost dagger (good steel) and **8 cp**.

### 9. THE LARDER / COLD-STORE
**Was:** the cold cellar — stone shelves, a meat-hook beam, kept cool for the manor's perishables.
**Now:** the goblins' food-hole. Stinking, hung with stolen brigand bacon and worse things best not named, the floor a midden. **Two to three goblins** are usually here squabbling over scraps.
> **Goblins (3):** AC 6, HD 1d8-1, **hp: 5, 2, 6**, arms: club/short sword 1d6, move 60 ft, morale 50.
In the muck, swept in with the leavings: **44 cp, 19 sp,** a tin spoon, and a **gold-washed reliquary locket** (looks fine, worth only 8 gp — gilt over lead — but the goblins prize it and Skritch will trade or fight for it).

### 10. THE BREWHOUSE
**Was:** where the grange brewed its small-beer and ale — a copper, a mash-tun, troughs.
**Now:** roofless and rain-rotted. The copper is gone (someone hauled it for scrap years ago); the tun is a heap of staves. **Stirges** have colonized the surviving rafter-stub and the dark of the old flue.
> **Stirges (4):** AC 8, HD 1+1, **hp: 6, 4, 7, 5**, attack 1d3 then attach & drain 1d4 blood/round, move 30 ft / fly 180 ft, morale 50.
Nothing of value but, fallen behind the broken tun and overlooked, a sealed **stoneware jug of brandy** (still good; a small luxury) and an iron pry-bar.

### 11. THE CHAPEL
**Was:** the small family chapel — a plain altar, a few benches, where the Greywethers kept their faith and buried their honored dead beneath the floor.
**Now:** roof partly fallen, the altar-stone cracked, pews long since burned for firewood. Three worn grave-slabs are set in the floor; Nan Tegg keeps them swept clean of leaves — the only tended thing inside the walls. Above the altar, a faded painted ram-and-barley device. Behind the cracked altar-stone, in a niche, the chapel's small treasures, never looted because nobody pious or curious has searched: a **plain silver chalice (40 gp), a censer (15 gp), and a string of prayer-beads with one real amber bead (20 gp).** Disturbing the grave-slabs upsets Nan Tegg greatly if she learns of it.

### 12. THE DOVECOTE
**Was:** the round stone dovecote — pigeons for the table and for messages, nest-holes ringing the inner wall.
**Now:** wild rock-doves and pigeons nest here still; the floor is deep in guano. A barn owl roosts at the top by day. Climbing the inner nest-holes to the top (a child could) reveals, wedged in a high hole where a long-ago servant hid it, a **small purse of 30 gp and a thin gold finger-ring (45 gp)** — somebody's secret hoard, the somebody long dead. The doves explode out in a clatter if disturbed (could draw attention — roll a wandering check).

### 13. THE BYRE / BARN
**Was:** the long timber barn and byre against the outer wall — winter shelter for sheep and the two oxen, hay in the loft.
**Now:** half-fallen, the loft a slope of rotten thatch and old hay. Bats roost in the rafters (harmless, but a clattering swarm if disturbed). The brigands stable **two horses and a mule** here under a sound corner of roof, with tack and a sack of oats; a brigand, **Cinch** (AC 8 leather, 1 HD, **hp 5**, spear 1d6), tends them and sleeps here. Buried in the old hay, a moldered drover's pack: a **whetstone, 6 sp, and a still-sound hand-axe.** The horses (worth 30-60 gp each in Wend) are the most portable treasure in the grange, and stealing them would enrage Mald more than the gold would.

### 14. THE OLD CISTERN
**Was:** a stone-lined rainwater cistern feeding the cold-store, off the cellar passage.
**Now:** half-full of black still water and dead leaves; the brigands and goblins both ignore it. A waterlogged corpse — a goblin that drowned or was drowned — floats face-down; on it, swallowed in the mud at the bottom (must wade/search the cold water), a brigand caravan's lost strongbox, never recovered: **inside, 300 sp, 80 gp, and two small amethysts (50 gp each).** Whether the goblin fell in reaching for it, or was pushed, is anyone's guess. The water is foul; drinking it risks sickness.

### 15. THE GOBLIN WARREN
**Was:** the deepest cellar room and the first of the natural caves the goblins broke into — never a finished room, just where the dug cellar met the living rock.
**Now:** the goblins' main den: filthy bedding, a smoldering dung-fire, bones, and the warren's whole rabble when not out. **Skritch** and the bulk of the goblins are here.
> **Skritch** (warren-boss): AC 6 (leather + buckler), HD 1d8-1 but rolled high, **hp 7**, short sword 1d6 + hand-axe, move 60 ft, morale 55. Carries a stolen **dagger with a jet-set pommel (worth 30 gp)** and a purse of **22 gp.**
> **Goblins (6):** AC 6, HD 1d8-1, **hp: 4, 6, 3, 5, 2, 7**, arms: 1d6, move 60 ft, morale 50.
The warren's hoard, in a hole under Skritch's bedding: **190 cp, 60 sp, 35 gp,** a copper armband (8 gp), and a child's silver bell-rattle (15 gp). Roughly half the goblins (3-7) may be out scavenging or sleeping in 9/16 at any time — adjust to taste.

### 16. THE NATURAL CAVE
**Was:** nothing made by hand — the natural cavern the warren spreads into.
**Now:** a damp limestone chamber, dripping, the goblins' overflow and rubbish-tip. A blind, milk-white cave-crawl of giant rats lives in the cracks.
> **Giant Rats (4):** AC 7, HD 1d4, **hp: 2, 3, 1, 4**, bite 1d3 (disease), move 120 ft, morale 50.
A side-crack too tight for a goblin but not for a determined human leads toward the fissure (17). Old mineral-stained bones — sheep, a dog, something larger — litter the floor. Nothing of worth but a dropped goblin necklace of pierced river-shells.

### 17. THE FISSURE  *(the strange thing's other mouth)*
**Was:** never anything — a natural rift in the rock that the old well-diggers broke into when they sunk the well "to the second water."
**Now:** a tall, narrow crack in the cave wall, cold air sighing out of it. Forty feet up the rift, the bottom of the **well-shaft (7)** opens — black water, a frayed rope just visible far above. The goblins will not enter here; they say it "talks." When the well whispers above, here the sound is louder, nearer, still wordless — and seems to come from no direction at all. **There is nothing to find and nothing to kill.** Crusts of bread (Nan Tegg's) lie at the edge. A character who waits, listens, calls into it may get... an answer, or an echo, or nothing — the DM never decides which it truly is. Leave it open. This is the module's one strange thing; resist every urge to staple a monster or a relic to it.

### 18. NAN TEGG'S BURROW
**Was:** a side-cellar, perhaps an ice-pit or root-store, dug off the undercroft long ago.
**Now:** the old graveward's nest — a pallet, a banked fire, dried herbs, a wall of small offerings (pebbles, a comb, a horn cup) laid before three little stone markers she has made for "the family." **Nan Tegg** lives here.
> **Nan Tegg:** non-combatant, AC 10, effectively 0-level, **hp 3.** No weapon but a paring-knife. If attacked she will only weep and curse; killing her gains the party nothing and costs them the best lore in the place.
She knows: who the Greywethers were; that the well was dug deeper "the year the whispering started, and after that the sheep sickened"; that the brigands fear the cellar and the goblins fear the fissure, "and only old Nan goes where she likes"; that there is "treasure the drowned man tried for" (rumor of 14); and that you mustn't take from the chapel-graves. Among her hoard of trinkets, unrecognized by her, a **real gold locket with a faded portrait (worth 50 gp)** — a Greywether heirloom she keeps "because the lady liked it." She will not part with it for coin, only perhaps for kindness, or a promise to tend the graves.

## MYSTERIES (no predetermined solution)

- **The well that whispers.** What is it? Wind, water-echo, the carried mutter of the goblins, the dead under the chapel, a spirit, nothing at all. The module does not know and neither should the DM. Different witnesses (Mald, Nan, Skritch) believe different things; none is confirmed. Let the players investigate and let the answer stay theirs.
- **Why the goblins fear the fissure but the brigands fear the well — the same hole, two ends.** Is it one thing felt from two sides, or two different fears? Unresolved.
- **The drowned goblin in the cistern.** Fell reaching for the strongbox, or pushed by another goblin who then couldn't reach it either? No clue confirms it.
- **Nan Tegg's loyalty.** Why does a woman with no memory of why still tend a dead family's graves? She cannot say. Leave it.
- **Whose bones are in the yard bramble and behind the cistern.** Creditors' hired men? Earlier squatters? The grange has eaten people quietly for seventy years.

## DM NOTES

- **The place does not care about the party.** It existed before them and is indifferent. There is no plot to foil, no villain to stop, no clock ticking toward catastrophe — only three sets of squatters minding their own survival and one strange sound nobody understands. Reward curiosity, caution, and good faction-play.
- **No right way through.** Players can sneak, parley, hire on with Mald, set the brigands and goblins against each other, befriend Nan Tegg for the lay of the place, raid the cellar from the well, or simply rob the dovecote and leave. All are legitimate. There is no intended route and no "solution."
- **Faction reactions.** Mald (room 4) parleys readily; he is short-handed and tired, and would sooner hire competent swords, sell information about the caravans, or strike a share-the-loot bargain than lose men. Threaten his people or his cache and he turns dangerous, but he runs before a hopeless fight. The goblins are cowardly and greedy: they ambush the weak, flee the strong, and can be bought off with food or shiny things, or turned against the brigands with a little cunning. The two groups keep an uneasy don't-poke-each-other truce that a clever party can break in either direction. Nan Tegg is no one's ally and no one's enemy — treat her gently and the grange opens up; harm her and you've thrown away its memory.
- **The strange thing stays strange.** Whatever the players decide the well is, the module never confirms it. If they "solve" it, that solution is theirs, not the text's. Do not reward them with a monster-in-the-hole or a relic; the reward for investigating the well is the investigating.
- **Treasure is here because people lost, hid, stored, or forgot it** — a card-pot abandoned mid-brawl, a servant's secret hole in the dovecote, a drowned strongbox, a hearthstone cache, an old family's chapel-plate nobody pious came back for. None of it was "placed for the heroes." Total coin/gems on the level is modest by design; the horses and the chapel-silver are the fattest easy prizes, and both come with strings.
- **Scale to taste.** Drop or add a brigand or two, shift goblins between 9/15/16, or skip the boar if the party is small. B1/B2 density: keep it tight and concrete.
